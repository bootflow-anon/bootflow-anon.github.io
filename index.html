Bootstrapped Generative Policies for Principled Exploration in Online Reinforcement Learning

Ant-v4

Abstract

Generative policies based on diffusion and flow matching deliver substantial gains on continuous control benchmarks, but every published flow- and diffusion-RL algorithm explores by injecting isotropic Gaussian noise or by inheriting SAC-style entropy regularization — an undirected scheme that wastes interactions on tasks with coordinated multi-joint control and early termination. We introduce BootFlow, the first ensemble-based exploration framework for generative RL policies. BootFlow pairs $K$ flow or diffusion policy heads with $K$ independent Q-networks, each trained on a bootstrapped subset of replay data. We show theoretically and empirically that shared Q-targets cause the heads to provably collapse to a single policy, while independent Q-targets preserve posterior diversity. On top of this architecture we introduce TIDE (Trajectory-Integrated Disagreement Exploration), an action-selection rule that scores candidates by $Q_\text{mean}(a) + \beta \cdot \Delta_\text{traj}(a)$, where $\Delta_\text{traj}$ aggregates inter-head disagreement across the entire denoising trajectory and $\beta$ is calibrated online from the running correlation between $\Delta_\text{traj}$ and the Q-standard-deviation, so the bonus is active only when the two signals agree. On five MuJoCo locomotion benchmarks — Ant, Walker2d, Hopper, HalfCheetah, Humanoid — BootFlow with flow matching and TIDE delivers a $6.6\times$ paired-mean improvement over SDAC on Ant-v4, with gains scaling with task exploration difficulty. Multi-seed ablations isolate the contributions of independent Q-targets, backbone choice (diffusion vs.\ flow matching), and exploration rule (Thompson, UCB, TIDE).

High-level idea

BootFlow couples ensemble exploration with the iterative structure of generative policies. At every environment step, $K$ independent actor–critic pairs each generate $M$ action candidates by running the underlying flow ODE (or diffusion reverse process) for $T$ steps. The TIDE rule then ranks these $K \!\times\! M$ candidates by combining (i) the ensemble Q-mean and (ii) the trajectory-integrated disagreement — the velocity-field standard deviation aggregated across all denoising timesteps, not just at the terminal action. Because trajectory disagreement is a tighter epistemic-uncertainty proxy than terminal disagreement (Proposition 1 in the paper), and because the bonus $\beta$ is gated by the running correlation between disagreement and Q-uncertainty, BootFlow explores aggressively where the ensemble is genuinely uncertain and backs off on unimodal tasks where it is not.

Methodology

Bootstrapped sampling. Each gradient update draws $K$ independent Bernoulli-masked subsets $\mathcal{D}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{D}_K$ from the replay buffer (mask probability $p \!=\! 0.8$). Each actor–critic pair sees a different $\sim\!80\%$ of the batch.

Per-head training. Each Q-network $Q_{\phi_k}$ is updated with a soft Bellman target that uses a REDQ-style 2-of-$K$ minimum over adjacent Q-networks; each policy $\pi_{\theta_k}$ is updated with a Q-weighted score-matching (diffusion) or flow-matching loss whose importance weights come from its own Q-network. Because every head optimizes a different Q-weighted objective, the heads are driven toward genuinely different optima rather than collapsing.

Action selection at interaction time. Every head generates $M$ candidates by integrating the flow ODE (or diffusion reverse process) for $T$ steps. The TIDE rule scores each candidate $a$ by $\,Q_\text{mean}(a) + \beta \cdot \Delta_\text{traj}(a)\,$, where $\Delta_\text{traj}$ is the inter-head velocity disagreement integrated over the entire denoising trajectory and $\beta$ is calibrated online by the running Pearson correlation $\rho(\Delta_\text{traj}, \sigma_Q)$. The candidate with the highest TIDE score is executed. The full per-step cost is one denoising/flow integration per head, which we measure in the paper's wall-clock table.

Learning curves

Training curves on five MuJoCo locomotion tasks (1M environment steps; 5M on Humanoid). Solid lines are the multi-seed mean; shaded regions are $\pm 1$ standard deviation across seeds $\{100, 200, 300, 400, 500\}$. BootFlow with either backbone clearly separates from SDAC on tasks that reward strategic exploration (Ant, Walker2d), matches SDAC on unimodal tasks (Hopper, HalfCheetah), and extends the ceiling on Humanoid. The bottom-right panel reports the aggregate normalized return averaged across all five environments after per-environment $[5\%, 95\%]$ percentile normalization, so high-scale environments do not dominate.

Ensemble diagnostics

Top row: mean head disagreement, defined as the per-dimension standard deviation across the $K$ policy heads evaluated at the terminal denoising step. Flow matching maintains $3$–$5\times$ higher disagreement than diffusion across every environment, confirming that flow matching supports a more diverse ensemble. Bottom row: Q-network divergence across the $K$ independent critics. Both backbones show the behavior predicted by the diversity-preservation theorem in the paper: Q-nets steadily diverge as training proceeds rather than collapsing onto each other — the failure mode that shared Q-targets would induce.
